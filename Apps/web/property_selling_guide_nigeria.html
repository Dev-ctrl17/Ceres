Navigating the Nigerian Legal Framework: Required Documentation

A property sale cannot proceed securely without immaculate title documentation. Regulatory due diligence in Nigeria is comprehensive, and minor document discrepancies can delay or entirely compromise a high-value transaction. When selling property in Nigeria, ownership must organize and present a verified dossier of legal titles:

  1. Certificate of Occupancy (C of O): The definitive land instrument issued directly by state governments, confirming land tenure rights for a designated 99-year duration.
  2. Deed of Assignment: The foundational transfer covenant executed between the vendor and the purchasing party, tracing historic ownership links and detailing the specific parameters of the current transaction.
  3. Approved Building Regulation Plans: Validated architectural clearances issued by municipal planning directorates, confirming the physical structure complies fully with state building zoning laws.
  4. Registered Survey Plans: Precise cartographic blueprints detailing the exact boundary coordinates of the land parcel, registered with the state Surveyor-General's office.
  5. Official Tax Clearance Certificates: Evidence verifying the selling party has fully resolved all outstanding personal or corporate tax obligations with relevant state revenue services.

Common Mistakes Sellers Must Avoid

Even seasoned property developers can encounter avoidable pitfalls that delay sales or diminish asset value. Review these frequent market errors to keep your transaction on a smooth, profitable track:

  • Neglecting Necessary Pre-Sale Repairs: Choosing to ignore noticeable structural or aesthetic flaws frequently alienates premium buyers, who may interpret deferred maintenance as a sign of deeper, hidden property issues.
  • Restricting Property Accessibility: Restricting viewing schedules reduces property exposure. Creating accommodating windows for verified buying agents and their clients dramatically accelerates deal velocity.
  • Overlooking Capital Gains Tax (CGT) Obligations: Failing to account for statutory tax and state processing fees can distort your net profit calculations. Always integrate these standard closing costs into your initial financial projections.

How to Close a Property Sale Successfully

The final closing phase requires meticulous attention to detail to ensure a secure, successful transition of asset ownership. Once financial terms are agreed upon, legal counsel coordinates the final exchange of contracts, securely processes payments via verified escrow accounts, and schedules the formal handover of all original title documents and physical keys.

Documenting a comprehensive structural inventory list signed by both parties prevents any post-closing misunderstandings. A seamless closing process leaves a lasting, positive professional impression, smoothing the way for future investment ventures.
